The U.S. Naturalization Process

Last updated July 2026 — based on USCIS's official “10 Steps to Naturalization”

Naturalization is the process by which a lawful permanent resident (Green Card holder) becomes a U.S. citizen. USCIS breaks the process into 10 general steps. Timelines vary a lot by field office and individual case, so treat the estimates below as rough guidance, not guarantees — always check your own case status through your USCIS account.

This page is a general overview, not legal advice. For guidance specific to your situation, consult the official USCIS 10 Steps to Naturalization page or an immigration attorney.

1. Determine Your Eligibility

Before applying, confirm you meet the general requirements: at least 18 years old, a permanent resident for the required number of years (usually 5, or 3 if married to a U.S. citizen), continuous residence and physical presence in the U.S., basic English ability, knowledge of civics, good moral character, and attachment to the principles of the U.S. Constitution.

2. Review the Naturalization Eligibility Worksheet

USCIS provides a worksheet to help you self-assess whether you're ready to apply before you commit time and the filing fee.

3. Complete Form N-400

Fill out Form N-400, Application for Naturalization — either online or on paper. Gather your supporting documents (Green Card, passport-style photos if filing from outside the U.S., and anything else the instructions require).

4. Submit Form N-400 and Pay the Fee

Submit your application and pay the filing fee (this amount changes periodically — always confirm the current fee on uscis.gov before paying). You'll receive a receipt notice you can use to track your case online.

5. Attend Your Biometrics Appointment

If required, USCIS will mail you an appointment notice with a date, time, and location for fingerprinting and a photo.

6. Complete the Interview

USCIS schedules an interview once your case is ready. You'll be asked about your application under oath, and you'll take the English and civics tests during this appointment (unless you qualify for an exemption).

7. Receive a Decision

USCIS mails (or posts to your online account) one of three outcomes:

  • Granted — your eligibility is established.
  • Continued — USCIS needs more evidence, or you didn't pass a portion of the test on the first try (you'll get a second chance, usually 60–90 days later).
  • Denied — the evidence didn't establish eligibility.

8. Receive Your Naturalization Ceremony Notice

If approved, some applicants can take the Oath of Allegiance the same day as their interview. Otherwise, USCIS mails a notice with your ceremony date, time, and location.

9. Take the Oath of Allegiance

You're not a U.S. citizen until you take the oath at a ceremony. At the ceremony, you'll turn in your Green Card, take the Oath of Allegiance, and receive your Certificate of Naturalization — check it carefully for errors before you leave.

10. Understand U.S. Citizenship

Citizenship comes with rights (like voting and traveling on a U.S. passport) and responsibilities (like jury duty and staying informed). This is also the point where studying for the civics test pays off beyond the interview itself — it's meant to reflect things every citizen benefits from knowing.

What If You Fail a Test Portion?

Failing doesn't mean your application is denied. You get two opportunities to take the English and civics tests per application. If you fail any portion at your first interview, USCIS schedules a second interview — typically 60 to 90 days later — to retest only the portion you missed.

Source: USCIS — 10 Steps to Naturalization
