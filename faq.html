Frequently Asked Questions

Last updated July 2026

Is the civics test multiple-choice?

No. The test is completely oral. A USCIS officer will read the questions aloud, and you must state your answer verbally. There are no multiple-choice options or word banks provided.

Which test version should I study, the 100-question or the 128-question pool?

It depends entirely on when you submitted your citizenship application (Form N-400):

  • If you filed before October 20, 2025: You will take the 2008 version (100 questions, up to 10 asked, need 6 right to pass).
  • If you filed on or after October 20, 2025: You will take the 2025 version (128 questions, up to 20 asked, need 12 right to pass).

This site asks for your filing date up front and automatically loads the right question set for you.

What happens if I make a mistake on a question?

Don't panic — the test simply continues. In the 2025 format, you can get up to 9 questions wrong before failing. The officer stops the test the moment you answer your 12th question correctly (or your 9th incorrectly). The 2008 format works the same way at a smaller scale: 6 correct to pass, and the officer stops once you've answered 6 correctly or 5 incorrectly.

Do some answers change based on where I live?

Yes. Questions about your U.S. Senators, U.S. Representative, and your state Governor depend entirely on your home address — there's no single correct answer we can show you. On this site, those answers appear in red text as a reminder to look up your own current officials rather than memorize a fixed answer.

What happens if someone fails the interview?

If you don't pass the civics test or the English test on your first try, your application isn't denied. USCIS will automatically schedule a second interview within 60 to 90 days. You'll only be re-tested on the specific portion you missed.

Are there any age exceptions for the test?

Yes. If you're 65 years of age or older and have been a permanent resident (Green Card holder) for 20 or more years, you qualify for the “65/20 exemption.” You only need to study a shortlist of 20 specific questions, and you can even take the exam in your native language with the help of an interpreter. This site asks about your age and years as a permanent resident up front, and offers a 65/20-only study mode automatically if you qualify.
